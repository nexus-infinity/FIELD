On 22 June (over fifteen weeks ago), the Commissioner announced on 3AW that he was looking into this issue.

On 29 June, VicRoads (confirmed in text response) that it was in consultation with the Commissioner.

On 27 July, the Commissioner formally announced an investigation had been initiated.

Fines Victoria put infringements on hold until September 30, pending the outcome of the investigation.

We know:

Ø Frankston Council representatives have indicated Council did not seek a new or revised approval for the plan when the extent of works changed significantly from the approved proposal,

Ø VicRoads has not provided any evidence of requiring a revised submission or issuing an updated approval,

Ø The installed signing included signs positioned so close to the Playne St intersection that motorists turning from Playne St could not see the signs,

Ø The section of the Highway between Davey St and Plowman St was not signed as a 40 km/h zone so motorists turning south from Davey St may have been unaware of the new limit

Ø Some advisory signing on intersecting streets was inadequate,

Ø No party is admitting responsibility for the 40 km/h sign installed on the northbound carriageway south of Davey St contrary to the interim proposal,

Ø Electronic speed limit signs were not provided, although this is a requirement of the DOT/VicRoads Speed Zoning Guidelines (Table 7),

Ø The communication requirements of VicRoads’ own Guidelines were ignored,

Ø The installation was not checked by the responsible authority – VicRoads,

Ø When the flaws with the signing were pointed out, VicRoads took several weeks to provide  temporary mobile electronic signs,

Ø The traffic profile was not monitored after the interim installation.

The changes when the original plan was installed on August 24 highlighted the deficiencies of the interim arrangement.

When speed limits are reduced on divided arterials, there must be an obligation on road authorities to ensure a high level of awareness by providing suitable signs, monitoring changes to traffic flows, and getting an independent road safety audit before enforcement action is taken.

It is not fair, reasonable, or responsible to do anything less in a shopping centre with high pedestrian activity. Road safety demands compliance with the DOT/VicRoads guidelines.

An independent audit (estimated cost approx. $5,000) would have shown issues with the planning, approval, installation, communication, and monitoring phases.

Speed cameras save lives, however, when operated where the signing is flawed, they can also generate large amounts of revenue.

It is time for answers.
